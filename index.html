Project Overview

This project analyses the IBM HR Employee Attrition dataset (1,470 employees) to uncover the factors behind employee turnover and build predictive models. It combines exploratory data analysis, preprocessing, and supervised machine learning to turn raw HR data into an actionable, decision-support tool — predicting who is likely to leave, explaining why, and recommending what HR can do about it.

01 — Introduction

The problem & why it matters

Problem statement

Employee attrition — people voluntarily leaving — is expensive. Replacing an employee costs time, money, and lost knowledge. If an organisation can identify who is at risk before they leave, HR can intervene early. This project frames that as a supervised binary classification problem: predict whether an employee's Attrition is Yes (will leave) or No (will stay).

Objectives

  • Explore the drivers of attrition through EDA.
  • Preprocess and prepare the data for modelling.
  • Train & compare multiple ML models.
  • Select the best model for the business goal.
  • Translate results into HR action.

Why it's challenging

The dataset is imbalanced — only ~16% of employees actually leave. A model that blindly predicts "everyone stays" would be 84% accurate yet useless. This shapes every modelling and evaluation choice.

02 — Exploratory Data Analysis

Understanding the data

From domain and dataset description, through preprocessing, to visual analysis and feature selection.

2.1 Domain & Dataset Description

The project uses the IBM HR Analytics Employee Attrition & Performance dataset — a fictional dataset published by IBM Watson Analytics and widely available on Kaggle, created to study the drivers of employee attrition.

  • DomainHuman Resources Analytics
  • Rows1,470 employees
  • Columns35 attributes
  • TargetAttrition — Yes / No
  • Class balance~16% left · ~84% stayed
  • Missing valuesNone

Target variable

Each employee is labelled Attrition = Yes (left) or No (stayed), mapped to 1 / 0 for modelling.

Feature types

A mix of numeric (age, income, tenure) and categorical (department, job role, marital status, overtime) attributes describing each employee.

2.2 Data Preprocessing

  1. 1

    Load & inspect

    Read the CSV into pandas (1,470 × 35); checked shapes, types, and confirmed zero missing values.

  2. 2

    Drop irrelevant columns

    Removed identifier/constant columns — EmployeeNumber, EmployeeCount, StandardHours, Over18 — that carry no predictive signal.

  3. 3

    Encode the target

    Mapped Attrition from Yes/No to 1/0.

  4. 4

    Label-encode categoricals

    Converted text categories to numbers, saving the encoders for consistent prediction later.

  5. 5

    Stratified train/test split

    80% train / 20% test with random_state=42, preserving the ~16% attrition ratio in both sets.

  6. 6

    Feature scaling

    Applied StandardScaler (fit on train only) for Logistic Regression; tree models use raw features as they're scale-invariant.

3.3 Model Selection

Why recall, and why these metrics

On an imbalanced problem, accuracy alone is misleading. We evaluated every model on accuracy, precision, recall, F1 and ROC-AUC. The deciding metric is recall — because missing an employee who's about to leave (a false negative) is far costlier than a false alarm. Among the simpler models, Logistic Regression gives the best recall-oriented balance and remains the most interpretable; the Stacking ensemble tops ROC-AUC and accuracy.

Takeaways

  • Boosting catches the most leavers (recall 0.81).
  • Stacking ranks risk best (AUC 0.82).
  • Logistic Regression stays the interpretable choice.
